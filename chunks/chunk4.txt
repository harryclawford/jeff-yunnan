8. ZHANG YIMOU'S IMPRESSIONS LIJIANG
================================================================================

--- WHO IS ZHANG YIMOU (張藝謀) ---

Zhang Yimou is arguably the most significant Chinese film director of the past
40 years. Born 1950 in Xi'an, he was persecuted during the Cultural Revolution,
entered the Beijing Film Academy in 1978 (first class after schools reopened),
and became part of the "Fifth Generation" of Chinese directors who transformed
Chinese cinema from Socialist Realist propaganda to internationally recognized
art.

His major films:
- Red Sorghum (1987): his directorial debut; won the Golden Bear at Berlin; bold
  colors, raw sexuality, violence — nothing like prior Chinese cinema
- Ju Dou (1990), Raise the Red Lantern (1991): female-centered stories of
  oppression in traditional Chinese society; both nominated for Academy Awards
- To Live (1994): Chinese life from 1940s through Cultural Revolution; banned in
  China for being too critical; won Cannes jury prize
- Hero (2002): wuxia epic with Jet Li; stunning color photography by Christopher
  Doyle; introduced Zhang to Hollywood-scale budgets and global release
- House of Flying Daggers (2004): operatic martial arts film
- 2008 Beijing Olympics Opening Ceremony: the defining moment of Zhang's mass
  spectacle aesthetic — 15,000 performers, synchronized at millimeter precision,
  watched by 4 billion people. His trademark: making human bodies into visual
  texture through choreographed mass movement.

His aesthetic signature: extreme color saturation, vast spaces with single human
figures, choreographed mass movement, images that read as paintings. He trained
as a cinematographer before directing, and it shows — his visual compositions
are fundamentally about light, color, and scale.

--- THE IMPRESSIONS SERIES ---

Zhang Yimou, Wang Chaoge, and Fan Yuefeng created a series of large-scale outdoor
performance spectacles starting in the early 2000s:
- Impressions Liu Sanjie (2004): Yangshuo, Guangxi, set on the Li River with karst
  mountains as backdrop — the first and still considered the best of the series
- Impressions West Lake (2007): Hangzhou
- Impressions Lijiang (2006): Jade Dragon Snow Mountain backdrop

All use:
- Natural landscape as stage backdrop (you literally cannot build a set like this)
- Hundreds to 500+ performers, predominantly local ethnic minorities
- Limited narrative: it's not a story so much as a sequence of images and
  impressions evoking culture and history
- Light design on a massive scale
- No spoken narrative (mostly instrumental and vocal music)

--- IMPRESSIONS LIJIANG: WHAT TO EXPECT ---

The stage is at ~3,100m altitude on the lower slopes of Jade Dragon Snow Mountain.
It's open-air, with the mountain behind the stage. Audience sits on elevated stands.

The performance is roughly 60-70 minutes. Thematic sections:
- The Tea Horse Road: hundreds of performers depicting caravan life
- Naxi religious ceremony: Dongba ritual elements
- The eternal love story: referencing the love suicide tradition (aestheticized)
- Mountain life: Naxi and other ethnic minority cultural practices
- Mass finale: the full cast, lit against the mountain

The production is deliberately spectacular rather than educational. It's closer
to a Beijing Olympics opening ceremony than a cultural documentary. Go in knowing
that — appreciate it as mass spectacle while you apply your independent knowledge
of what it's representing.

One observation: the performers are mostly Naxi and other local ethnic minority
people — this is real employment and has provided income for local families. The
relationship between Zhang Yimou's commercial spectacles and cultural authenticity
is debated, but the economic impact on performer communities is genuinely positive.

The show runs at sunset. The fading light behind the mountain during the
performance is legitimately spectacular. Bring a warm layer — at 3,100m, post-sunset
temperatures drop fast even in March.

9. PU'ER TEA PRIMER
================================================================================

--- WHY YUNNAN, SPECIFICALLY ---

Tea (Camellia sinensis) was domesticated in Yunnan. The oldest wild tea trees in
the world — some over 1,000 years old, a few claimed to be 2,700+ years old —
grow in the ancient tea forests of southern Yunnan (Xishuangbanna, Lincang, Pu'er).
These ancient trees (古樹 gǔshù, "ancient tree") produce leaves with a different
chemical profile from plantation-grown tea: more complexity, lower bitterness,
different aging potential.

The altitude and climate of Yunnan produces tea leaves with high polyphenol content
and specific secondary compounds that make Yunnan tea behave differently from Fujian,
Zhejiang, or Taiwanese teas under fermentation and aging. This isn't marketing —
it's chemistry. Pu'er specifically requires these Yunnan varietals (called "large-
leaf varietal" — 大葉種 dàyèzhǒng) to behave the way it does during fermentation.

--- SHENG (生) VS SHOU (熟): THE FUNDAMENTAL DISTINCTION ---

Sheng Pu'er (生普洱 — Raw/Green):
- Fresh Yunnan leaves are processed minimally (fixed with low heat to stop
  oxidation, then sun-dried), then compressed into cakes or bricks
- Initially tastes green, grassy, bitter, astringent — somewhat like a very
  intense green tea
- Ages over years/decades through natural microbial fermentation (similar principle
  to how aged wine works, but with different chemistry)
- As it ages: bitterness reduces, complexity increases, color deepens from green
  to red-brown, flavors develop toward earthiness, dried fruit, leather, forest floor
- Good aged sheng (10-20+ years) is to good young sheng as aged burgundy is to
  nouveau beaujolais — it's a transformation, not just time passing
- The most valuable pu'er on earth is aged sheng — factory-produced cakes from
  the 1950s-1970s sell for tens of thousands of USD

Shou Pu'er (熟普洱 — Ripe/Cooked):
- Invented in 1973 by the Kunming Tea Factory to simulate the results of years
  of aging in months
- Fresh leaves undergo accelerated fermentation through "wò duī" (渥堆) —
  piled in a humid room with controlled temperature for 45-60 days, with microbial
  activity simulating natural aging
- Result: a tea that starts dark, earthy, and mellow — no aging wait needed
- Tastes smooth, dark, often described as "forest floor," "dark chocolate,"
  "prune," or "dried fruit" depending on quality
- Shou never develops the same complexity as well-aged sheng, but is more
  approachable and consistent

Analogy that may help: sheng pu'er is to shou pu'er as unfiltered natural wine
that ages beautifully is to a young wine processed to be immediately drinkable.
Both are wine; one has the potential for complexity through time that the other
skips.

--- THE AGING PROCESS AND WHAT "VINTAGE" MEANS ---

Pu'er cakes are stamped with production information: producer, grade, year.
The year matters differently than wine vintage — because fermentation continues
indefinitely (given proper storage), older doesn't automatically mean better.
Storage conditions are the variable:
- 乾倉 (gān cāng / dry storage): cool, dry, slow fermentation. The gold standard
  for collectors. Preserves the tea's character while allowing gradual transformation.
- 濕倉 (shī cāng / wet storage): humid, faster fermentation. Faster aging but
  can introduce musty, moldy flavors if poorly managed. Previously more common in
  Hong Kong storage. Detectable by a slight "wet cardboard" smell.

A 15-year-old dry-stored sheng from reputable provenance is worth dramatically more
than a 15-year-old wet-stored cake from the same year.

--- WHAT TO LOOK FOR WHEN BUYING ---

Quality indicators:
1. Leaf quality: open the wrapper (if permitted), look for intact large leaves with
   intact veins. Powdery debris suggests low-grade processing or poor storage.
2. Compression: firm but not concrete-hard. Should be possible to pry leaves with
   a tea needle (店家 should have one). Too loose = moisture issues during storage.
3. Smell the dry cake: clean, aromatic (young sheng), or complex/earthy (aged).
   Any mold, chemical, or "closed" smell = storage problems.
4. Ask to taste: any serious shop will brew for you. A single steep will tell you
   whether the tea has balance.
5. Provenance claims: be skeptical of anything claiming famous mountain origins
   (冰島, 老班章, etc.) at tourist-market prices. These village-specific teas
   command premiums because of limited supply; genuine examples don't end up in
   tourist street shops at ¥200/cake.

Price reality check in Lijiang:
- Reasonable everyday sheng/shou: ¥60-150/cake (357g standard)
- Decent quality, some age: ¥200-500
- Proper aged sheng, verifiable provenance: ¥500-3,000+
- Anything called "30-year aged" for under ¥800 is almost certainly lying
- Buy a few small samples of different types rather than one large purchase until
  you find what you like

The honest advice: buy what you can taste and verify, not what the story is.
Stories are free; tea quality is not.

10. PRACTICAL CULTURAL TIPS
================================================================================

--- ALTITUDE AWARENESS ---

Lijiang: 2,400m. Shuhe/Shaxi: approximately 2,100-2,200m. Jade Dragon Snow Mountain
cable car top: 4,506m. Tiger Leaping Gorge viewing area: approximately 1,800-2,400m.

2,400m is real altitude — not dangerous for most healthy adults, but you'll notice:
- Slightly faster heartbeat and breathing, especially on stairs
- Possible headache on Day 1-2 (drink extra water, no alcohol Day 1)
- Sleep quality may be slightly worse the first night
- Sun at altitude is more intense — UV exposure is higher, wear sunscreen

The cable car to 4,506m is the most significant altitude exposure. Ascent is rapid
(from ~3,100m base in 20 minutes), giving your body no time to acclimatize.
Symptoms at the top: headache, slight nausea, dizziness. These are normal and
temporary. Walk slowly. Sit if you feel dizzy. Oxygen canisters help.
Don't attempt to exert yourself at that altitude.

Practical: carry paracetamol (helps altitude headache), stay hydrated, limit alcohol
especially the first two days. Acetazolamide (Diamox) is the medical prevention
but requires prescription and advance preparation.

--- VISITING ETHNIC MINORITY HOMES (YUHU VILLAGE HOME VISIT) ---

- Remove shoes at the entrance threshold if there's a clear transition from outside
  to interior. When in doubt, look at whether your host's shoes are on or off and
  follow their lead.
- Accept tea and food offered. Refusing is mildly rude — a polite initial refusal
  followed by acceptance is fine ("Please, don't trouble yourself" + accept) which
  mirrors local social convention. Outright refusal signals contempt.
- Do not touch religious objects (altars, incense burners, ancestor tablets) unless
  invited to.
- The hearth/kitchen area in traditional homes is often spiritually significant.
  Don't treat it as background scenery for photos.
- Gifts: if visiting, small gifts are appreciated. Local food items (dried fruit,
  packaged snacks) or small money gifts to children are appropriate. Don't gift
  clocks (implies death in Chinese culture).

--- PHOTOGRAPHY NORMS ---

- Always ask before photographing individuals, especially older people and minorities
  in traditional dress.
- The Mandarin phrase: "我可以給您拍照嗎？" (Wǒ kěyǐ gěi nín pāizhào ma?) —
  "May I take your photo?"
- Many people will say yes. Some older Naxi women in full traditional dress in
  tourist areas expect a small payment for photos (¥5-20 is normal). This is
  a legitimate economic arrangement, not exploitation.
- In religious spaces (temples, active ritual areas): no photos during active
  ceremony. In general temple spaces, ask or look for signs. Flash photography
  is universally unwelcome near aged paintings or murals.
- The Baisha Murals: no flash. Some sections may be off-limits. Respect the
  restrictions — these are genuinely irreplaceable.

--- HAGGLING CULTURE ---

- Haggling is normal in markets and street stalls, mildly expected in souvenir
  shops, NOT appropriate in tea shops with fixed pricing or restaurants.
- How to haggle well: know what you want to pay before starting, make a counter-
  offer about 40-50% below asking (expect to meet somewhere in the 60-70% range),
  be pleasant — it's a social transaction, not combat. Walking away is legitimate
  negotiation; following up and completing the sale is fine.
- Don't haggle over food prices or basic services. Only appropriate for goods.
- In tea shops: prices in reputable shops are generally set. Asking for a small
  discount on a larger purchase is fine. Asking to halve the price on a tea cake
  marks you as unknowledgeable and wastes everyone's time.

--- GENERAL ETIQUETTE ---

- "给我..." (Gěi wǒ...) is grammatically correct but sounds rude — it's "give me."
  Use "请给我..." (Qǐng gěi wǒ...) — "please give me" or learn to point + "谢谢"
- Tipping: not conventional in mainland China. Tips in tourist areas are sometimes
  accepted but not expected. Guide tips at the end of a multi-day service are
  appropriate (¥50-100/day is reasonable for excellent service).
- Pointing at people with a single finger is considered rude. Use an open hand.
- Complimenting someone's home or possessions: usually fine. Complimenting children
  excessively is done with a superstitious caveat in traditional families (can
  "invite evil eye") — but this is less observed in modern Yunnan.

--- FOOD SAFETY ---

- Street food: safe at busy stalls that serve locals. Look for high turnover.
  Less busy tourist-oriented stalls: more risk.
- Mushrooms: eat at reputable restaurants only. Don't forage yourself.
- Tap water: don't drink it anywhere in Yunnan. Bottled water is cheap and available
  everywhere. Use bottled water for brushing teeth if you're cautious.
- Spice levels: Yunnan cuisine is less spicy than Sichuan but spicier than you
  might expect. "不要太辣" (Bù yào tài là) = "not too spicy" is useful.
- Altitude affects alcohol absorption — you'll get drunk faster at 2,400m.
  Yunnan grain wine (白酒 báijiǔ) will be offered; sipping is fine, pounding is not.

11. CONVERSATION STARTERS
================================================================================

These are questions that signal genuine curiosity and will open up better
conversations with guides and locals. They're designed to get past the rehearsed
tourist explanation and into real knowledge.

--- FOR NAXI GUIDES OR LOCALS ---

"Is the Dongba script still taught to children? Can you read it, or is it only
Dongba priests who learn it today?"
→ Opens up: the revival vs. extinction debate, which young Naxi care about
  cultural preservation vs. which don't, how schools approach it.

"The Naxi Ancient Music uses instruments that are extinct in the rest of China.
Who decides what's authentic? Are there debates among Naxi musicians about
how to perform it?"
→ Opens up: cultural ownership, the tension between preservation and performance,
  how ethnic identity is performed vs. lived.

"After the 1996 earthquake — did people in the old town think about rebuilding
in modern materials? What made them choose to rebuild the same way?"
→ Opens up: attachment to place, economic calculations (tourism vs. function),
  government incentives, personal stories of loss and rebuilding.

"The love suicide tradition — is that something that's still openly discussed
in Naxi families, or is it more something people know about historically but
don't talk about?"
→ Opens up: generational attitudes to tradition, how Naxi people feel about
  tourist romanticization of something that was a tragedy.

"Joseph Rock lived here for decades. Do people here have stories passed down
about him from their grandparents?"
→ Opens up: family memories of the Rock period, how locals viewed this foreign
  researcher, what he got right and wrong.

--- FOR BAI GUIDES OR LOCALS ---

"The Battle of Tali in 754 — Tang dynasty lost catastrophically to Nanzhao.
Do people here still feel pride about that? Does it feel like local history or
distant history?"
→ Opens up: ethnic minority relationship to Han Chinese historical narrative,
  whether Bai identity is defined against Han-ness or alongside it.

"The Catholic church in Dali — are there still Bai Catholic families? Do they
experience tension between being Catholic and being Bai, or has it become
just part of Bai identity?"
→ Opens up: syncretism, religious identity, French missionary legacy.

"The tie-dye workshop — is this something young Bai women learn? Are there
masters who can do the really complex traditional patterns, or is that
knowledge in danger of being lost?"
→ Opens up: craft economics, apprenticeship, whether traditional skills survive
  in the tourism economy or are killed by it.

"Erhai Lake — what was it like 20 years ago versus now? Do people think the
cleanup has worked?"
→ Opens up: environmental change, local vs. government priorities, lake fishing
  culture that has changed.

--- FOR TEA SHOP OWNERS ---

"If you had a friend who was new to pu'er and could only buy one cake to start
learning — what would you give them and why?"
→ Good tea people will love this question. It reveals their pedagogy.

"What's the difference between a tea from a tree that's 100 years old and one
that's 500 years old — can you actually taste the difference?"
→ Opens up: gǔshù mythology vs. reality, whether tree age is auditable,
  honest shop owners will admit the premium may exceed the sensory difference.

--- FOR ANYONE ALONG THE TEA HORSE ROAD ---

"If the Tea Horse Road was operating today — what would the modern equivalent
be? What would Yunnan be trading with its neighbors?"
→ Gets locals thinking about their region's economic position today, Yunnan's
  role in China's Belt and Road, whether they feel connected to that larger story.

"The road took caravans months to complete. What's the fastest you can get
from here to Tibet today? Does that change how people here think about Tibet —
as close or far?"
→ Opens up: infrastructure change, cultural attitudes to neighboring regions,
  what "near" and "far" meant before roads.

--- UNIVERSAL GOOD QUESTION ---

"What do most tourists miss about this place — something important that they
walk right past or never think to ask about?"
→ Every knowledgeable local has an answer to this. It signals you're not a
  typical tourist. You'll almost always get something genuinely interesting.

FINAL NOTE
================================================================================

You're visiting a place where the history isn't over. The Tea Horse Road caravans
stopped in the 20th century but the routes remain. The Naxi are navigating
between cultural preservation and modernity in real time. The glaciers on Jade
Dragon Snow Mountain are measurably smaller than when Joseph Rock photographed
them a century ago. The dam that would have flooded Tiger Leaping Gorge was
stopped by activism that's still remembered. The Catholic church in Dali is
still receiving congregation.

Everything you'll see has layers. The tourist surface is just the beginning.
Pull on any thread and you'll find a story that connects to something bigger —
a trade empire, a defeated dynasty, a script that survived when it should have
died, a mountain nobody has ever stood on.

That's Yunnan. It rewards the curious more than almost anywhere else.
