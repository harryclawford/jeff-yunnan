5. JADE DRAGON SNOW MOUNTAIN (玉龍雪山)
================================================================================

--- THE GEOLOGY ---

Jade Dragon Snow Mountain (5,596m, Yulong Xueshan) is not a volcano. It's
a fault-block mountain — pushed upward by the same tectonic collision that
created the Tibetan Plateau and, locally, carved Tiger Leaping Gorge. The
uplift is geologically recent and rapid (in geological terms); the mountain is
still rising. The thirteen peaks are the exposed granite/limestone spine of this
upthrust block. The glaciers on the north face are genuine remnants of a larger
glacial system from the last ice age.

Those glaciers are retreating significantly. The main accessible glacier (White
Water Terrace area and the cable car terminus area at ~4,506m) was once
considerably larger. Chinese government climate studies have documented major
volume loss. The glacial water feeds the Yulong River, which feeds the Naxi
irrigation systems and, eventually, joins the Jinsha. The ecological chain from
glacier → river → agriculture → civilization is direct and visible.

--- SACRED SIGNIFICANCE TO THE NAXI ---

Jade Dragon Snow Mountain is home to Sanduo (三多) — the war god and protective
deity of the Naxi people. Sanduo manifests as a white warrior on a white horse
from the mountain. In Naxi mythology, the mountain itself IS a deity, one of the
sons of the Jade Emperor who descended to protect the Naxi from enemies. The Mu
clan chiefs made annual offerings to the mountain.

The sacred status explains why the mountain has NEVER been summited. Not because
it's technically impossible (it's challenging but not like Everest) — it's because
the Naxi consider any attempt to stand on the summit a desecration. Chinese
mountaineering authorities have respected this prohibition. This makes Yulong
Xueshan one of the world's most significant "off-limits" peaks on religious grounds.
Compare this to Uluru in Australia, where the Anangu Aboriginal people similarly
ask visitors not to climb the rock (Australia finally banned climbing in 2019).

--- THE LOVE SUICIDE TRADITION ---

This is the aspect of Naxi culture that shocked Western researchers when first
documented and still requires careful explanation.

In traditional Naxi society, marriages were arranged by families for economic
and political alliance. Young people who fell in love outside these arrangements
had limited options. The Naxi developed a cultural practice (not universal, but
documented and named) called "going to the jade realm together" (殉情 xùnqíng):
couples who faced impossible obstacles to their love — usually family opposition
to a match — would go to the mountains (often to JDSS), dress in their finest
clothes, write letters, and die together. The belief: in the realm beyond life,
they would be free to be together without the constraints of family and society.

This is NOT a romantic fantasy to the Naxi — it was a genuine social tragedy that
reflected the collision of arranged marriage systems with individual romantic love.
Ethnographers who documented this in the early 20th century found that the practice
was correlated with periods of social stress and economic hardship. It declined
as Naxi society modernized and marriage became more self-determined.

The importance of understanding this: when you see references to Naxi love
tradition, "eternal love on the mountain," or similar romanticized framing in
tourist materials, you're seeing a tragic social phenomenon aestheticized for
consumption. The actual history is more complex and more interesting.

--- VISITING THE PARK ---

The cable car (索道) takes you to approximately 4,506m. At that altitude:
- Altitude sickness is a real risk — headache, nausea, dizziness
- The temperature at the top is roughly 15-20°C colder than Lijiang
- Walk slowly. Breathe deliberately. Don't run.
- Oxygen canisters are sold at the top — useful to have one, not embarrassing
- The view on clear days extends across the entire plateau, with Tiger Leaping
  Gorge visible as a distant cleft to the north

The Blue Moon Valley (lower elevation) and the Glacier Park (higher, cable car)
are separate ticket zones. Budget time and energy accordingly.

6. TIGER LEAPING GORGE (虎跳峽)
================================================================================

--- THE NUMBERS THAT PUT IT IN PERSPECTIVE ---

Tiger Leaping Gorge is one of the deepest gorges on Earth. The numbers:
- Width at narrowest point: approximately 30 meters (river surface)
- Total depth from water to summit of Jade Dragon Snow Mountain: ~3,900 meters
- For comparison: the Grand Canyon is ~1,800m deep at its deepest
- The gorge is roughly 16km long

The Jinsha River (金沙江 — "River of Golden Sand") runs through it. The Jinsha
IS the upper Yangtze River — it becomes the Yangtze proper downstream near
Yibin in Sichuan. The name "Golden Sand" comes from the yellow-brown sediment
visible in the water. Standing at the gorge, you're looking at one of Asia's
great river systems in its most dramatic setting.

--- THE GEOLOGICAL FORMATION ---

The gorge was carved by the Jinsha River cutting downward as the Tibetan Plateau
rose upward — a process still ongoing. The river is essentially running in place
while the mountains rise around it. This is called antecedent drainage: the river
existed before the mountains, and it cuts downward as fast as the ground rises,
maintaining its course. The same process created most of the deep gorges in
western Yunnan and Tibet.

The rocks exposed in the gorge walls include extremely ancient metamorphic
basement rocks — among the oldest surface rock visible in this region, brought
to the surface by tectonic uplift from depths where they formed billions of years ago.

--- THE LEGEND ---

The gorge's name comes from a local legend: a tiger, pursued by a hunter, leaped
across the narrowest point of the gorge using a mid-river boulder as a stepping
stone. The "Tiger Leaping Stone" (虎跳石) is visible in the river at the lower
gorge viewpoint — a large boulder that would, hypothetically, make such a jump
geometrically possible. The legend is Naxi in origin, and the tiger (Naxi: döqöq)
is an important cultural symbol.

The real significance of the legend: it communicates how impossibly narrow the
gorge is. If a tiger can jump it, the gorge is merely tiger-jumpable. The legend
is a scale indicator as much as a story.

--- THE DAM CONTROVERSY ---

In the early 2000s, plans were advanced for a hydroelectric dam on the Jinsha River
that would have flooded the lower gorge and required relocation of roughly 100,000
people from valley communities. The dam project triggered one of China's most
significant environmental activism campaigns — led by journalists, NGOs, and
local communities — and was eventually suspended in 2007 (though not permanently
cancelled). The campaign was documented in the film "Waking Up the Tiger" and
marked a turning point in Chinese environmental advocacy.

The gorge's continued existence as you see it is partly the result of that advocacy.
Worth knowing when you stand there.

--- VISITING THE GORGE ---

The upper gorge is accessible from the road; the famous "Tiger Leaping Stone"
viewpoint is a short walk. The upper hiking trail (high trail, 2-day route) is
a legendary trekking route but you won't have time for it. Even the road viewpoints
give an adequate sense of scale. Morning light hits the Jade Dragon Snow Mountain
side first; afternoon light hits the Haba Snow Mountain (哈巴雪山) side on the
opposite bank.

March: water levels are relatively low (dry season), so the river is calmer than
summer monsoon flows. The contrast between the tranquil-looking narrow river and
the scale of the surrounding mountains is at its most dramatic in clear dry-season
weather.

7. DALI & THE KINGDOM OF NANZHAO
================================================================================

--- THE KINGDOM THAT FOUGHT OFF TANG DYNASTY CHINA ---

To understand how significant this is, you need context on Tang China. The Tang
dynasty (618-907 AD) was arguably China at its most powerful and internationally
connected — a cosmopolitan empire with its capital Chang'an as the world's largest
city, projecting military power across Central Asia, Korea, and Vietnam. Tang armies
were generally not easily stopped.

Nanzhao (南詔, 649-902 AD) stopped them.

The kingdom was founded by the Cuan and later the Meng clan — ethnic Bai and Yi
mixed ruling class. They unified six smaller chiefdoms and by the mid-700s were
a serious regional power. Their capital was at Taihe, south of modern Dali,
near the southern shore of Erhai Lake (you can visit ruins nearby).

The Battle of Tali (750 and 754 AD): Tang general Xianyu Zhongtong led campaigns
against Nanzhao after it allied with Tibet. The first invasion ended in defeat.
The second, in 754 AD, saw Tang general Li Mi lead an army estimated at 60,000-
100,000 men — and Nanzhao + Tibetan forces destroyed it almost entirely. Chinese
historical records (and the poetry of Du Fu, who wrote about the conscripted
soldiers and their families' grief) suggest this was one of the most catastrophic
military losses in Tang history.

The historical significance: this victory, combined with the An Lushan Rebellion
(which started in 755 AD, one year later), marked the beginning of Tang decline.
Some historians argue that the Nanzhao campaigns severely depleted Tang military
resources at exactly the wrong moment. The Battle of Tali may have changed the
trajectory of Chinese imperial history.

--- NANZHAO'S CULTURAL SOPHISTICATION ---

Nanzhao was not a "barbarian" kingdom. It maintained diplomatic relationships with
Tang China even while defeating its armies, sent students to study in Chang'an,
adopted Buddhist religion (the Three Pagodas date to this era), and produced its
own literature and art. The kingdom's rulers styled themselves as bodhisattvas —
Buddhist enlightened beings — which gave them legitimacy beyond mere military power.

The Three Pagodas of Chongsheng Temple (崇圣寺三塔) outside Dali are the most
visible legacy of Nanzhao/Dali Kingdom Buddhist culture. The main pagoda (Qianxun
Pagoda) is 69m tall, built 823-840 AD during Nanzhao. The style shows Tang
influences (the Nanzhao court absorbed Tang aesthetic), but the scale and survival
of the complex demonstrate serious state investment in Buddhist architecture.

--- THE DALI KINGDOM (937-1253 AD) ---

After Nanzhao collapsed (internal power struggles), the Dali Kingdom emerged under
the Duan clan — another mixed Bai-Yi ruling house. Dali Kingdom was MORE Buddhist
than Nanzhao, more peaceful, and traded extensively rather than fought. The
Buddhist painting traditions that produced the Baisha Murals trace back to Dali
Kingdom patronage.

The Dali Kingdom lasted over 300 years and maintained independence when the Song
dynasty to its north chose not to attempt conquest (Song emperors had enough
problems with Jurchen Jin in the north and didn't want another Nanzhao campaign).

--- THE MONGOL CONQUEST (1253 AD) ---

Kublai Khan ended Dali Kingdom with a brilliant strategic move. Instead of attacking
Song China directly from the north (heavily fortified), he decided to FLANK it —
sending his general Uriyangkhadai southwest through Tibet and then south to conquer
Dali Kingdom. The Dali Kingdom surrendered with relatively little fighting (the last
Duan king cooperated and was kept as a local governor).

This Mongol conquest of Dali was a strategic stepping stone: it gave the Mongols
(later Yuan dynasty) a southwestern base from which to eventually encircle Song
China from three sides. The fall of Dali effectively sealed Song's eventual fate.
The Duan family, interestingly, continued as hereditary governors of Yunnan under
Mongol/Yuan and then Ming dynasties — one of the longer-running aristocratic
continuities in Chinese history.

--- WHY IS THERE A CATHOLIC CHURCH IN DALI? ---

(Covered in D6 section above, but to recap and expand):

French MEP missionaries arrived in Yunnan in the 19th century via the connection
to their missions in Vietnam (which became a French protectorate after 1883). The
Bai people were receptive to conversion in some communities — partly because
Buddhism in the region had become associated with the Dali Kingdom ruling class,
and some Bai communities had more fluid religious identities, practicing folk
religion blended with Buddhism. Catholicism offered an alternative identity, plus
the practical benefits of missionary education and medical care.

The Dali church (St. Joseph's Catholic Church) was built in 1927, designed to
blend Bai and European architectural elements. The result: a Catholic church with
curved Bai-style rooftiles, local stone construction, and a European Gothic window
pattern. The fusion is genuine, not touristy — it reflects the actual process of
cultural negotiation between Bai converts, their local builders, and French priests.

During the Cultural Revolution (1966-1976), the church was seized and used as a
granary, factory, or dormitory (common fate for religious buildings). It was returned
to the Catholic community and restored in the 1980s.
