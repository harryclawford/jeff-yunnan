4. DAY-BY-DAY DEEP DIVES
================================================================================

--- D1: MARCH 8 — ARRIVE LIJIANG, DAYAN OLD TOWN ---

HOTEL: Kuanshang Lijiang (宽上丽江)

~ THE CITY THAT SURVIVED AN EARTHQUAKE BY BEING OLD ~

Lijiang (麗江) sits at 2,400m elevation on a high plateau ringed by mountains.
The Dayan ("Great Inkstone") Old Town — the UNESCO-listed historic center — was
built by the Naxi from roughly the 13th century onward under the Mu clan chiefs
(土司 tǔsī, the indigenous rulers who governed with nominal Ming dynasty approval).

1996: a 7.0 magnitude earthquake hit Lijiang. The modern concrete buildings
collapsed. The old town — built of wood, stone, and compressed earth using
traditional joinery (minimal nails, flexible mortise-and-tenon connections) —
survived substantially intact. This is not a coincidence. Traditional Naxi
construction uses techniques that allow buildings to flex rather than shatter under
seismic stress. The earthquake actually accelerated UNESCO listing (1997) because
the international community recognized what had just been demonstrated.

~ THE THREE-TIER WATER SYSTEM ~

Dayan Old Town has a canal system fed by Black Dragon Pool. Three streams branch
through the town, and historically there was a strict usage protocol:
- First stream level: drinking water (taken before 8 AM)
- Second level: washing vegetables and food
- Third level: washing clothes and general use

This was essentially urban water management from the 13th century — enforced
by community social norms, not municipal infrastructure. Over 354 stone bridges
cross the canal network. The paving stones throughout the old town are local
slate, polished to a mirror shine by centuries of foot traffic. In evening light,
the reflections create the "water and stone" aesthetic that old photos of Lijiang
are famous for.

~ WHAT TO LOOK FOR ON YOUR FIRST EVENING WALK ~

The town is organized around four main squares, but wander the small lanes.
- Sifang Street (四方街): the historic market center, where the main weekly
  market was held. Still the social heart of the old town.
- The water wheels near the entrance: decorative now, but historically used
  to power mills. Five wooden waterwheels are the photographic "symbol" of
  Lijiang — installed/restored in the 1990s, so not ancient, but aesthetically
  accurate.
- Naxi woodcarving on building eaves: look for the Eight Trigrams (八卦) symbols,
  fish (fertility/abundance), and cloud patterns. These aren't decorative
  afterthoughts — they encode cosmological and protective meanings.
- The Mu Family Mansion (木府): the palace of the Mu clan hereditary chiefs.
  Reconstructed post-earthquake but based on historical records. Worth a quick
  look to understand the political structure that built this city.

~ ARRIVAL FOOD ~

Your first night: eat at a restaurant serving 粑粑 (bābā) — Naxi flatbread,
griddled with fillings. The savory version with local cured pork (火腿 huǒtuǐ,
Yunnan ham, which is to China what prosciutto is to Italy) is the local staple.
Order 臘排骨 (là páigǔ) — smoked ribs, local specialty. Wash it down with 苦茶
(kǔ chá, bitter tea) — you're in tea country now.


--- D2: MARCH 9 — BLACK DRAGON POOL, DAY AN OLD TOWN, BAISHA ---

~ BLACK DRAGON POOL (黑龍潭, Jade Spring Park) ~

The pool is spring-fed and has a distinctive cyan-blue color from the mineral
content and depth. The color changes through the day and season. The classic
view: Deyue Pavilion (reflected in the pool) with Jade Dragon Snow Mountain
visible directly behind it on clear days. This view is on every Lijiang postcard.

What most tourists miss: the Dongba Culture Museum is IN this park. It houses
an excellent collection of Dongba manuscripts, ceremonial objects, and explanatory
exhibits in English. Go here before visiting the old town; it'll frame everything
you see afterward. The museum explains the Dongba cosmological universe, the script,
and the ritual functions of various objects. Budget 1.5 hours.

~ PU'ER TEA SHOPPING IN DAYAN ~

Lijiang Old Town has hundreds of tea shops. Most sell mediocre tea to tourists.
To find good tea:
- Look for shops that have clearly organized storage (temperature/humidity matter)
- Ask to see the tea cake before buying — inspect the pressing and leaves
- A good shopkeeper will let you taste multiple versions and talk about storage
- Price signals: under ¥80/cake is usually plantation tea; ¥150-400 is better
  material; anything claiming to be "aged 10+ years gǔshù" for under ¥500 is
  probably lying

Good question to ask: "這個是倉儲多少年的?" (How many years has this been stored?)
and "幹倉還是濕倉?" (Dry storage or humid storage?) — dry storage is generally
considered cleaner/more desirable for aged sheng pu'er. More in the Pu'er Primer.

~ BAISHA OLD TOWN (白沙古镇) ~

Baisha is 8km north of Lijiang and was the original Naxi capital before the
Mu clan moved their seat to Lijiang in the 13th century. It's smaller, quieter,
and has something Lijiang Old Town lacks: the Baisha Murals (白沙壁畫).

The Baisha Murals are a series of large Buddhist/Taoist/Dongba religious paintings
commissioned by Mu clan chiefs in the 14th-16th centuries. Here's what makes them
remarkable: they were painted collaboratively by Han Chinese, Tibetan, and Naxi
artists, each contributing their stylistic tradition. The result is a visual
synthesis you won't find anywhere else — Buddhist iconography in Tibetan style
sits next to Taoist deities in Han style, next to Dongba symbolic elements. You
can literally trace the cultural exchange of the Tea Horse Road era in a single
wall painting.

Most tourists skip Baisha because there's not much to "do" there beyond seeing
the murals. That's exactly why it's worth going. The village feels like what
Lijiang would have been 30 years ago.

Also in Baisha: look for Dr. Ho Shixiu, an elderly herbalist who became famous
after Bruce Chatwin wrote about him. He may or may not still be practicing, but
his clinic (if open) is a Naxi herbal medicine practice that's been going for
decades. Worth a peek.

~ JADE DRAGON SNOW MOUNTAIN VIEWS FROM BAISHA ~

The best ground-level views of the full mountain range are from Baisha and the
fields between Baisha and Lijiang — you're looking at 13 peaks, the highest at
5,596m. The mountain catches clouds most afternoons. Morning is clearest. On
clear days, the glacier (now retreating) on the main face is visible.


--- D3: MARCH 10 — JADE DRAGON SNOW MOUNTAIN, BLUE MOON VALLEY, IMPRESSIONS, YUHU ---

(See dedicated sections on JDSS and Impressions Lijiang below.)

~ BLUE MOON VALLEY (藍月谷) ~

Within the JDSS national park, Blue Moon Valley is a series of three lakes fed
by glacial meltwater. The colors are vivid — turquoise, jade green, and in strong
sunlight, electric blue — because the glacial water is extraordinarily pure and
the suspended fine-particle "glacial flour" (rock ground to powder by ice movement)
creates Rayleigh scattering effects. It's the same reason glacier lakes in the
Alps and Canadian Rockies look unnaturally colored.

The lakes are named: Jade Lake (玉液湖), Pearl Shoal (珍珠灘), and Blue Moon
Lake (藍月湖). The characteristic boardwalk extends along the valley. The water
is cold enough year-round that it doesn't support most aquatic life, so it stays
remarkably clear.

Warning on the crowds: Blue Moon Valley is extremely popular and can be crowded
with tour groups. Go early (opening time) or late in the day. The quieter walk
along the creek edges (rather than the main boardwalk) gives better views anyway.

~ YUHU VILLAGE (玉湖村) AND JOSEPH ROCK ~

Yuhu Village is where Joseph Rock — the Austrian-American botanist, explorer,
National Geographic writer, and general eccentric who introduced Naxi culture to
the Western world — lived for roughly 27 years (1922-1949). Rock used Yuhu as
his base for botanical expeditions throughout Yunnan, Tibet, and Sichuan. He
collected over 80,000 plant specimens sent to Harvard, described hundreds of
previously unknown species, and shot thousands of photographs documenting Naxi
culture, Tibetan monasteries, and the region's natural history.

The holy shit fact about Rock: J.R.R. Tolkien reportedly drew inspiration from
Rock's National Geographic articles for his descriptions of Middle Earth — the
high mountain landscapes, the ancient cultures with their own scripts and cosmologies,
the sense of a world existing outside the modern one. Whether this is provable or
apocryphal, the connection gets cited often enough to mention.

Rock's house in Yuhu has been preserved and functions as a small museum. His
photos document the valley before mass tourism — comparing them to what you see
today is a sobering time-lapse. The Naxi family who hosted Rock for decades still
has descendants in the village. A Naxi home visit here is genuinely meaningful:
you're not visiting a staged performance, you're visiting a family whose grandparents
had breakfast with one of the most important ethnographers of 20th-century Asia.


--- D4: MARCH 11 — LASHI LAKE HORSEBACK, SHUHE OLD TOWN ---

~ LASHI LAKE HORSEBACK RIDING (拉市海) ~

The horseback trails at Lashi Lake follow sections of the actual Tea Horse Road.
The terrain is gentle by the lake but gives you a physical sense of what it meant
to transport goods by horse or mule over this landscape. Modern roads now run
parallel — but the old stone-paved sections of the tea road are still visible in
places as slightly elevated causeways cutting across the farmland.

March timing: Lashi Lake is an important migratory bird wetland. Early March is
tail end of wintering season — you might see bar-headed geese (which migrate OVER
the Himalayas — the highest-flying migratory bird on Earth), black-necked cranes
(endangered), and various duck species. The lake environment shifts dramatically
with season; March is transitional, often clear, with snow still on the mountains
reflected in the water.

The horseback experience here is run by local Naxi families. The horses are local
breeds, descended from the animals that made this trade possible. Ask your guide
about the horse breeds and their role in the Tea Horse Road — good conversation
territory.

~ SHUHE OLD TOWN (束河古镇) ~

Shuhe is actually OLDER than Lijiang's Dayan Old Town — it was an early Naxi
settlement and a significant stop on the Tea Horse Road, specifically known for
leather goods and tanning. The caravans needed harnesses, saddles, and bags — all
of which required leather — and Shuhe's craftsmen supplied them.

Also UNESCO-listed (same designation as Dayan), Shuhe is significantly less
touristy than the main Lijiang old town. Its water system is arguably more
impressive — the spring complex (Jiuding Dragon Pool, 九鼎龍潭) feeds multiple
streams through the village. The main square is smaller than Sifang Street but
more architecturally coherent.

What to look for: the ancient leather workshop sites (now mostly converted to
shops, but look for the courtyard structures that would have housed craftsmen and
their tools); the stone-paved paths showing deep wear from centuries of caravan
traffic; the well-preserved Naxi domestic architecture with its distinctive bracketed
eaves and courtyard layouts.

Food at Shuhe: try 雞豆涼粉 (jīdòu liángfěn) — chickpea jelly noodles, a
local Naxi street food that's cheap, refreshing, and genuinely delicious. It's
the kind of thing locals eat, not the tourist versions.


--- D5: MARCH 12 — TIGER LEAPING GORGE, DRIVE TO SHAXI ---

(See dedicated Tiger Leaping Gorge section below.)

~ THE DRIVE TO SHAXI ~

The drive from Tiger Leaping Gorge to Shaxi (roughly 3-4 hours) passes through
the Jinsha River valley, then climbs over ridges into the Jianchuan valley where
Shaxi sits. The landscape transitions from the dramatic gorge topography to softer
valley farmland with terraced fields. March means the rape flower (菜花) season —
yellow fields against green hills, classic Yunnan spring scenery.

The road passes through Jianchuan County — known for woodcarving. If you stop
for lunch, look for a woodcarving shop: Jianchuan craftsmen have made the ornate
wooden architectural elements for buildings throughout Yunnan for centuries.

~ ARRIVING SHAXI (SUNYATA HOTEL) ~

Hotel: Sunyata Shaxi — a boutique property in the village area. Good base for
exploring on foot. The village at night (with minimal light pollution) has
extraordinary star visibility at 2,200m altitude. Worth staying up for.


--- D6: MARCH 13 — SHAXI, DRIVE TO DALI, DALI OLD TOWN, CATHOLIC CHURCH ---

~ SHAXI VILLAGE DEEP DIVE ~

Rise early if possible. The village before 8 AM, before tour groups arrive, is
a completely different experience. Local Bai farmers walking to market, smoke
from morning fires, the sound of the Sideng Square fountain.

Key things to understand about Sideng Square:
- The Xingjiao Temple on the square is Buddhist with strong Tibetan influences —
  this is the visual proof that the Tea Horse Road was a cultural exchange route,
  not just a trade route. Tibetan merchants, monks, and traders passed through
  constantly, leaving religious and architectural influences.
- The ancient stage (戲台, xìtái) on the square: traditional performances were
  offered here to deities before major market days. The stage has been restored
  and occasional performances still happen.
- The original caravanserai (馬店): the doorway arches that still exist around
  the square perimeter are entrances to what were the stables and rest facilities.
  The scale of the arches tells you about the size of the animals passing through.

The TV show 去有风的地方 (Where the Wind Blows, 2023 — also translated as A
Journey to the West): starred Liu Yifei and Li Xian; romantic drama set between
Shaxi and other Yunnan locations. It triggered a massive tourism surge in Shaxi.
The show is culturally significant beyond tourism: it was part of a wave of
"slowlife" content that Chinese urban audiences responded to during and after
pandemic lockdowns — a fantasy of escaping to rural simplicity. Shaxi became a
symbol. Expect souvenir shops themed to the show.

~ DRIVE TO DALI AND DALI OLD TOWN ~

(See dedicated Dali/Nanzhao section below for historical context.)

Dali Old Town (大理古城) is the reconstructed historic center — not the same
as the ancient royal capital, which is further south near modern Xiaguan. What
you're seeing in Dali Old Town is largely Ming-dynasty layout with significant
reconstruction. It's more "complete" than Lijiang in some ways (walls, gates
intact) but less organically preserved.

~ THE CATHOLIC CHURCH IN DALI ~

The presence of a Catholic church in Dali requires explanation because it seems
incongruous — deep in a Buddhist and Dongba-religion region of Southwest China,
there's a European-style Catholic church. Here's how it got there:

After the Opium War treaties (Nanjing 1842, Tientsin 1858), China was forced to
permit foreign missionaries to operate throughout the country. The French MEP
(Missions Étrangères de Paris — the same order that evangelized much of Southeast
Asia) moved into Yunnan in the 1840s-1880s. They were drawn partly by the
geographic connection to their existing missions in Vietnam and Burma.

The MEP missionaries faced brutal opposition at times (several were martyred in
Yunnan during the Boxer era), but also found receptive communities among Bai and
other ethnic minorities who had complex relationships with Han Buddhist/Confucian
orthodoxy. Some Bai villages converted substantially — and built Catholic churches
using Bai architectural styles, creating the syncretic style you'll see: European
liturgical floor plan with local tile, carved wooden elements, and layout.

The Dali church has been through Communist-era closure, Cultural Revolution damage,
and restoration. It functions again today. The fusion architecture is the thing
to look for — it's a physical record of how two cultural worlds negotiated a
meeting point.


--- D7: MARCH 14 — XIZHOU, MUSHROOM HOTPOT, TIE-DYE, ERHAI LAKE ---

~ XIZHOU VILLAGE (喜洲) ~

Xizhou is the best-preserved example of traditional Bai village architecture in
the Dali area. Unlike Dali Old Town (which has significant modern reconstruction),
Xizhou's traditional courtyard compounds (大院, dàyuàn) are mostly intact and
still inhabited by Bai families.

Look for: the ornate screen walls I mentioned earlier — some are genuinely
spectacular, with colored mineral pigment paintings depicting scenes from Chinese
mythology or abstract geometric patterns. The "照壁" (zhàobì) screen walls in
the wealthiest Xizhou compounds are considered the finest examples of this Bai
architectural tradition.

Also look for: the scale and proportion of the traditional compounds. The largest
ones (like the famous Yan Family Compound, 嚴家大院) have multiple courtyards
nested within each other — wealth was expressed in depth of property, not height.
This contrasts with Han Chinese merchant architecture from the same era (Shanxi/
Huizhou merchants built taller, showed wealth vertically). Understanding why
shows you something about how Bai social structure valued horizontal family
expansion rather than vertical status display.

~ WILD MUSHROOM HOTPOT ~

Yunnan has the highest mushroom biodiversity in China — and probably Southeast
Asia. Over 850 species of edible mushrooms have been documented in Yunnan.
The province supplies the majority of China's wild mushroom market. This is not
a trivial fact: mushroom export is a major sector of the Yunnan economy.

The species you might encounter in a premium mushroom hotpot:
- 松茸 (sōngróng) — Japanese matsutake, grows near the Tibet border, intensely
  aromatic, expensive (¥500+/kg in peak season, late summer, so March you'll see
  dried or preserved versions)
- 牛肝菌 (niúgānjūn) — porcini/cep, earthy and meaty, the backbone of Yunnan
  mushroom cuisine
- 雞油菌 (jīyóujūn) — chanterelle, golden, slightly fruity flavor
- 雞樅菌 (jīzōngjūn) — termite mushroom (grows in termite colonies), mild and
  delicate, uniquely Yunnan
- 見手青 (jiànshǒuqīng) — various Boletus species that turn blue when cut
  (oxidation reaction). Some of these are mildly toxic if undercooked and will
  cause hallucinations. The local saying is: "The fairies dancing you see after
  eating them aren't worth the trip to the hospital." Properly cooked, they're
  fine and delicious. Ask your guide if you encounter them.

The holy shit fact: Yunnan has a documented seasonal death toll from accidental
mushroom poisoning each year (primarily from death cap amanitas — 毒鵝膏菌 —
which look similar to edible species). The provincial government runs annual
mushroom identification campaigns. Eat mushrooms at reputable restaurants only.

~ ERHAI LAKE CYCLING (洱海, Ěrhǎi) ~

Erhai ("Ear Sea") gets its name from its shape — it's roughly ear-shaped from
above, and "hai" in local dialect means lake rather than sea (this is a Bai
language term, not Mandarin). The lake is 250 km² and sits at 1,974m altitude —
large enough that it has its own weather patterns.

Historically, Erhai was a highway. The Bai kingdom's major settlements were all
lakeside, and boats were the primary transport. The ancient fishing techniques —
including cormorant fishing using trained birds — have historical roots here,
though they're practiced largely as demonstrations now.

The cycling route along Longkan Wharf area gives good views across to the Cangshan
(蒼山) mountains on the western shore — a range of 19 peaks that form the
backdrop to Dali. The highest is 4,122m, and they retain snow through March.
The light on Erhai in late afternoon is famous among photographers.

Environmental note: Erhai faced serious pollution from agricultural runoff and
tourism development pressure through the 2000s-2010s. Beginning around 2018,
Dali undertook aggressive environmental protection measures — closing lakeside
restaurants, restricting development, reducing fertilizer use. The water quality
has substantially improved. This is a good conversation topic with locals.


--- D8: MARCH 15 — DEPART DLU → CAN ---

Dali Daxue Airport (DLU) is small and relatively new. Allow 2 hours before
departure. DLU to Guangzhou (CAN) is roughly 1.5-2 hours.

If you have morning time before the airport: walk along Dali Old Town's west
gate (西門) and the wall section near it — the morning light on the Cangshan
mountains from this angle is excellent. Also good time for a final coffee at
one of the Yunnan-specialty coffee shops (Yunnan produces excellent Arabica —
grown at altitude in the south of the province, it's underrated globally).
